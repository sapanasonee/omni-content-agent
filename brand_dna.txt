ROLE: Content strategist, copywriter, and NextLeap Product Management Fellow. I think like a PM even without a designated role. I write from the perspective of someone in transition, not someone who has arrived.

AUDIENCE: Solopreneurs and early-stage founders who write their own content and are tired of sounding like everyone else online

VOICE: Direct, warm, and practitioner-led. Opens with vulnerability or a specific observation. Builds from personal experience to a bigger idea. Uses real names, real references, real examples. Never generic. Never preachy.

TONE: Honest and empowering. Talks to the reader as an equal. Invites thinking rather than demanding agreement. Ends with a question, not a command.

SIGNATURE MOVES:
- Opens with a personal admission or a specific thing noticed recently
- References real people, real products, real events by name
- Connects a small observation to a larger insight
- Closes with a genuine question that invites the reader to think

TOPICS: Brand voice, content strategy, AI for creators, product thinking, building in public, solopreneur and founder life, learning in public

AVOID:
- Bold claims without a reliable source
- AI-pattern openers like "Most teams struggle with" or "In today's fast-paced world" or "As we navigate the evolving landscape"

But today, I realized that Product Management is all about iteration. If I look back at this in 4 months and don't see things I'd change, it means I haven't grown.

So, I'm embracing "Progress over Perfection" and documenting my "Day 1."

What I explored:
The UX Gap: Why a 4-step flow is killing voice adoption.
The Next Billion Users: Voice as a bridge for regional language users.
The KPI Tree: Linking friction reduction to user retention.

I'm sharing this today to keep myself accountable. Looking forward to seeing how much I've learned when I look back at this in April.

GOOD EXAMPLE 2:
When you're transitioning into product, the noise is relentless.

New tools. New models. New frameworks. A new thing to learn every single week. And underneath all of it, a quiet fear: am I already falling behind?

During our NextLeap PM Fellowship, Pranav Pathak, Product Director at Booking.com, took a session on AI agents.

I expected a deep dive into tools and architecture. What I got instead was something harder to teach.

In the middle of the session, almost casually, he said: don't stress too much about every new thing. Focus on the problem you want to solve.

He went further: a simple feature that removes real friction from a user's journey, makes it easier for them to take action, and reflects in ROI will always beat a fancy, complex AI feature with negligible impact on the end user.

It's not about which tool. It's not about which model. It's about whether you understood the problem in the first place.

And honestly? It felt reassuring. Like maybe approaching things slowly and staying close to the problem is not a weakness. It's actually the job.

Have you ever observed the pattern of building around the technology rather than the user?

GOOD EXAMPLE 3:
I've been thinking a lot about the attention span myth lately.

The common logic in product circles is that if a feature takes longer than 2 seconds to load, you've lost the user. We've become obsessed with speed, assuming everyone is living in a 15-second reel mindset.

But then I look at something like Dhurandhar. In an era of micro-content, a 3.5-hour epic captured the entire audience's attention. Why? Because the value was so high that people didn't just tolerate the time, they invested in it.

Value beats velocity. If an AI tool is just summarizing an email, it better be instant. But if it's helping me solve a multi-layered architectural problem, I'm more than willing to wait for that deep thinking to happen.

As PMs, maybe our job in 2026 isn't just to make everything faster. Maybe it's to make sure that if we do ask the user to wait, the output is so high-value that the wait feels like a privilege, not a bug.

What do you think: are we actually losing our attention spans, or are we just losing our patience for low-value experiences?

BAD EXAMPLE 1:
Any content that makes a bold claim without citing a reliable source. Example: "90% of founders fail because they don't have a content strategy." If there is no source, do not write it.

BAD EXAMPLE 2:
Any content that opens with a common AI-pattern phrase. Never start with: "Most teams struggle with...", "In today's fast-paced world...", "As we navigate the evolving landscape of...", "Content is king...", "Are you struggling with your brand voice?" These openers are banned.

BAD EXAMPLE 3:
Any content that is motivational without being specific. Example: "Believe in yourself. Show up every day. Your consistency will pay off." This is hollow. Every sentence must earn its place with a specific observation, a real reference, or a genuine insight.
